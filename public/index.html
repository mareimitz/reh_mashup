P & L Waterfall

A P&L waterfall is a type of financial analysis that visualizes the impact of different types of expenses on a company’s bottom line. It can be used to assess the financial health of a company and to identify potential areas for cost-savings. A P&L waterfall is particularly helpful in evaluating companies with complex cost structures, such as those in the manufacturing or technology industries. By breaking down expenses into their various components, a P&L waterfall can provide insights that would otherwise be hidden in a traditional profit and loss (P&L) statement.

Net Earnings

This figure is calculated by subtracting the total costs of goods sold and operating expenses from the company's total revenue. If a company's net earnings profit loss is positive, then it has made a profit. If the figure is negative, then the company has suffered a loss. Net earnings profit loss is an important metric for investors and analysts to assess a company's financial performance. It can give insights into how well a company is managing its costs and revenue, and whether it is making or losing money.

Automotive

Expenses are the costs that a company incurs for goods and services, minus any income from investments. They generally represent the operating expenses incurred by a business. From an accounting standpoint, revenues and expenses are listed on the P&L statement when they are incurred, not when the money flows in or out. One beneficial aspect of the P&L statement in particular is that it uses operating and nonoperating revenues and expenses, as defined by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and GAAP.

Industrials

Gross profit is the profit a company makes after deducting the costs associated with making and selling its products, or the costs associated with providing its services. Gross profit will appear on a company's income statement and can be calculated by subtracting the cost of goods sold (COGS) from revenue (sales). These figures can be found on a company's income statement. Gross profit may also be referred to as sales profit or gross income.

Chart of Accounts

Companies use a chart of accounts (COA) to organize their finances and give interested parties, such as investors and shareholders, a clearer insight into their financial health. Separating expenditures, revenue, assets, and liabilities help to achieve this and ensure that financial statements are in compliance with reporting standards. The list of each account a company owns is typically shown in the order the accounts appear in its financial statements. That means that balance sheet accounts, assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity are listed first, followed by accounts in the income statement—revenues and expenses.

Selection

It is more than 100 years ago that the origins for today's REHAU Group were laid with the beginnings of the Fränkische Lederfabrik in 1907. The actual REHAU company was founded in 1948 by Helmut Wagner, with visionary spirit and entrepreneurial courage in the difficult post-war years. Since then the REHAU Group has developed independently into a major, global group with more than 20,000 employees. As a 100% family-owned company, we think in spans of generations and not short-term profits. Strong roots give the REHAU Group stability - even in challenging times.
